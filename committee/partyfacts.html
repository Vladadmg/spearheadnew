How Much for a Good Time?
or
The Financial Truth about the Super Party
by Priscilla Lore

It’s never too early to start planning the next Super Party, and your Committee has already been discussing who, what, where, when. The last party was such a success that we want to make sure everyone has just as good a time this year—or even better. But, along with the rave reviews of the 2005 party, we heard several objections to having to pay $5 to attend. With all the bake sales, the garage sale, and donated items, we were asked, shouldn’t the party be free? Well, yes, it should—but, alas, it’s not possible. The Committee thought it a good idea to break down the actual costs for the party well in advance of the next one because, frankly, we’re expecting to have to raise the cost of admission from $5 to maybe $10 or even more. Bear with me and I’ll explain why.

It may look like a balanced budget…

In 2005 we raised more than $800 from the garage sale, nearly $3,000 from the five bake sales, and $750 from the $5 admission for 150 partygoers. That added up to $4518.50 raised and we were bucks up for a while—not bad! Party expenses, however, made short work of that: more than $4,100 for the catering (Intimate Catering—weren’t they great?), $225 for the beautiful Golden Gate Yacht Club (and on a pretty, clear evening—thanks over and over again to Sharon Castle), another $75 for the busboys, and nearly $60 for soft drinks. This added up to $4,521.53, or an actual cost of $30.10 per person—we broke even, and that’s with the $5 charge. Looks perfect, right? EXCEPT that several critical items were donated last year and we can’t count on those donations in our plans for 2006.

… But we’d be lost without donations

Included in the donations were bottled water, bar snacks before dinner, the beautiful flowers and candles, and the printing of the programs. Our talented entertainment—Matthew Treviño, Brian Frutiger, and accompanist Donald White—were each paid $100, which was generously donated; additional Yacht Club expenses of $500 were also donated. Also, to cut costs, we served wine left from last year’s party (cellared in the fabled Opera House caves). NOT TO MENTION the donation of time, energy, and creativity on the part of everyone involved. (Cost breakdown was provided by Barbara Sherman in a report she presented to the Committee and graphs were created by Paul Szczesiul.)

As you can see, the Committee did everything possible to keep costs down and still arrange a rockin’ party. Bear in mind that these are not the only expenditures the Committee makes over the year. We also provide candies for supers on opening night of their show, pay modest website expenses to maintain SpearheadNews.com, reimburse Tom Carlisle for maintaining the elaborate and beautiful super scrapbooks, as well as purchase postage for the few mailed items, and keep a good store of supplies for the bake sales (plates, utensils, etc.). With all these expenses we’re not exactly rolling in dough. For the 2006 party, we are expecting the cost for catering, which is the bulk of the outlay, to go up. We also anticipate cost increases in most other areas as well.

So where’s the money going to come from?

It became clear during the Committee’s party postmortem meeting that we need to raise more funds to keep the party at the level we enjoyed so much last year. Among our choices are to have more bake sales, charge more per item at the bake sales, raise the price of admission to the party, and solicit supers for additional donations.

See why we have to charge admission to the party?

Even if you think you shouldn’t have to pay it, you have to admit that $5 for an evening of talented singers, great food, beautiful setting, and the company of fellow supers all dressed up (some in costume!) was a small price to pay. Well worth twice that, if not more.

Talk to us

Your Committee wants the entire super community to feel represented, so please send your thoughts and suggestions on the party and other expenses. It’s your party, after all, and your feedback is important.
